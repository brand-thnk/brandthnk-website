Website Audit

Digital Onboarding, Inc. | Comprehensive Website Analysis | March 16, 2026

Project Overview

digitalonboarding.com | Prepared by BrandThnk

Scope: Full website audit using StoryBrand SB7 framework, competitive positioning analysis, and conversion optimization assessment.

Methodology: Site crawl combined with Fintech Competitive Intelligence Dashboard (SimilarWeb, LinkedIn Ad Library, Meta Ad Library).

Executive Summary

Key Findings

digitalonboarding.com has strong product-market fit buried under weak storytelling. The site describes features clearly. It does not describe the buyer's problem at all. Every page leads with what Digital Onboarding does rather than what the buyer is struggling with. That distinction matters because it determines whether a first-time visitor stays for 10 seconds or 10 minutes.

The competitive intelligence dashboard makes the stakes plain. DO's organic traffic declined 29.3% year-over-year. The site pulls roughly 4,179 monthly visits against nCino's 69,541. There is near-zero paid search spend while Narmi has increased theirs by 548%. Every visitor who lands on this site is disproportionately valuable, and right now the site is not converting that scarce traffic into pipeline.

The site's biggest structural problem is that it positions Digital Onboarding as the hero of the story. StoryBrand's SB7 framework teaches the opposite: the customer is the hero, the brand is the guide. A community bank CMO running a four-person marketing team who just lost 15% of new accounts to dormancy should land on this homepage and immediately feel understood. Instead, they land on three rotating taglines ("Activate relationships. Deepen engagement. Amplify growth.") that could belong to any martech vendor in the country.

There is also placeholder text on the homepage awards section that needs attention. That tells a prospect something about quality control.

Two additional gaps compound the problem. First, DO's leadership team (Ted Brown, Nate Shahan, Adam Westley) is invisible on the site. The About page lists names and titles. It does not position these people as practitioners with a point of view on data-driven engagement, lifecycle orchestration, or the future of digital banking.

Second, the site does not tell a data story. DO's platform is built on first-party behavioral data, automated targeting, and lifecycle engagement across digital banking channels. None of that comes through in the messaging. The word "data" barely appears on the homepage. "Lifecycle" does not appear at all.

StoryBrand SB7 Framework Analysis

Assessment of digitalonboarding.com against Donald Miller's seven-part narrative structure

Donald Miller's StoryBrand framework maps brand messaging to a seven-part narrative structure. When a website follows this structure, visitors immediately understand three things: what is the problem, how does this company help me solve it, and what does my life look like after. When it doesn't, visitors get feature lists and jargon. Here is how DO's site performs against each element of the SB7.

SB7 Element Current State Assessment
1. Character (the hero) Absent The hero should be the buyer: a CMO or VP Digital at a community bank/CU with a small team and big goals. The homepage never names this person. It opens with DO's capabilities, not the buyer's situation.
2. Problem Partially present The Onboarding page mentions the 2x2x2 failure and dormancy. But the homepage does not name the problem clearly. "Struggling to retain account holders?" appears only on the Platform Overview page, not above the fold on the homepage where it belongs.
3. Guide Weak A guide demonstrates empathy and authority. The site has authority signals (170+ FIs, case study metrics, awards) but they're scattered. Empathy is almost entirely missing. Critically, none of DO's leaders (Ted, Nate, Adam) appear as guides on the site beyond an About page headshot grid.
4. Plan Overcomplicated StoryBrand says: give the hero a simple plan (3 steps). The site offers a maze: Platform Overview, Data & Targeting, Analytics, Setup & Support, Use Cases, Pricing, four Solution pages, and a widget library. A first-time visitor cannot figure out where to go.
5. Call to Action Present but soft "Let's Talk" is the primary CTA. It appears in the nav and on multiple pages. The conversion page itself is clean (Marketo form + ChiliPiper calendar). But there is no transitional CTA: no gated benchmark report, no ROI calculator, no self-assessment tool.
6. Failure Absent StoryBrand says: show the stakes. What happens if the buyer does nothing? DO knows the answer: dormancy, silent attrition, competitor poaching, wasted acquisition spend. The site never says this directly on the homepage.
7. Success Partially present The case study metrics are strong: 375% more direct deposits, 64% reduction in attrition, 800% growth in online banking enrollments. But they appear as a scrolling ticker, not as a narrative. A visitor needs to see themselves in the transformation.

SB7 Verdict

The site is organized around what DO sells rather than what the buyer needs. Rewriting the homepage through StoryBrand's lens is the single highest-ROI change available because it is the first thing every visitor sees, regardless of how they got there.

Page-by-Page Audit

Homepage

First impression (5-second test): A visitor sees three rotating taglines ("Activate relationships. Deepen engagement. Amplify growth."), an awards banner, and a ticker of case study metrics. The taglines are internally focused verbs. They describe what DO wants to happen, not what the buyer wants to happen.

Hero section: No customer-facing language above the fold. Compare to Narmi, whose homepage leads with a specific promise to a named buyer segment. Compare to Fenergo, who leads with a compliance pain point. DO's hero section reads like a brand guidelines exercise, not a sales page.

Placeholder Content: There is live placeholder text on the homepage in the awards section followed by a generic "Button Text" CTA. This is a quality control failure visible to every visitor.

Platform Overview Page

Opening: "Struggling to retain account holders? You're not alone." This is the best empathy line on the entire site. It should be on the homepage, not two clicks deep. The rest of the page shifts immediately into feature descriptions and widget inventories.

Content structure: The page is extremely long. It cycles through: engage everywhere, popular campaigns, features, widgets, testimonials, case studies, and more features. A visitor scrolling through this is doing work the site should do for them.

Customers Page

Assessment: This is the strongest page on the site. The metrics are specific, named, and credible. Sound Credit Union (7% text conversion rate, 3x industry average), Island FCU (25% drop in checking attrition), Leader Bank (27% reduction in escrow calls), NYU FCU (10% of at-risk accounts saved).

Problem: This page is one click deep in the navigation and the stories are not repurposed anywhere else on the site. Every one of these metrics should appear on the homepage, on the relevant solution page, and in paid ad copy.

Competitive Positioning: Where the Site Falls Short

Your competitive intelligence dashboard reveals three patterns that directly inform what this site needs to do differently:

  • nCino's content machine is producing at scale. 1,013 active LinkedIn ads, 69,541 monthly visits, +122.9% organic growth. Their website is a conversion engine backed by massive paid amplification. DO cannot match this spend. But DO can match the messaging discipline.
  • Narmi's proof-point strategy is the playbook to study. Narmi runs 101 LinkedIn ads and every single one features a named client with a specific metric ($22M in new deposits in 120 days, 89% digital adoption in 90 days, 50% less support volume).
  • Fenergo's fear-based hook converts. Fenergo leads with "$1.9B in EMEA regulatory penalties" and "93% of FIs are planning to adopt agentic AI." Fear and urgency work in financial services when backed by third-party data.

Start / Stop / Continue

Prioritized by effort-to-impact ratio. The first three STARTs are the highest-leverage moves.

START

New actions. Ordered by impact.

S1
Rewrite the homepage hero through StoryBrand
Lead with the buyer's problem (disconnected data, dormant accounts, underused digital banking). Name the hero (community bank/CU marketing leader with a small team). Position DO as the data-driven engagement guide.
Medium (copy + design, 2-3 weeks)
S2
Fix the placeholder text and "Button Text" placeholder
This is live on the production site in the awards section. It signals to every visitor that the site is not maintained.
Trivial (30 min)
S3
Build dedicated profile pages for Ted, Nate, and Adam
Each page should feature their POV on data-driven engagement, a 60-second video, links to their authored content, and a booking link. People productization starts on the website.
Medium (content + design, 2-3 weeks)
S4
Create a transitional CTA
A gated benchmark report authored by Nate or Ted ("2026 Data-Driven Onboarding Benchmarks: What 170+ FIs Taught Us About Lifecycle Engagement"). Place it on every page.
Medium (content + design, 3-4 weeks)

STOP

Patterns actively hurting engagement and conversion.

X1
Stop leading with capability language
These taglines describe what DO wants. StoryBrand says: open with what the customer wants. The customer wants fewer dormant accounts, connected data, and lifecycle engagement that works inside their digital banking channel.
X2
Stop treating DO's leadership team as an afterthought
The About page has a headshot grid with no POV, no authored content, and no way to hear from Ted, Nate, or Adam directly.
X3
Stop describing the platform as an "onboarding" tool
The messaging limits DO to one moment in the lifecycle. The site should position DO as a data-driven engagement platform that spans the full account holder lifecycle.

CONTINUE

Working. Do more.

C1
Customer video testimonials from named executives
Named executive video is the highest-trust content format in B2B fintech. DO has four. Most competitors have zero. Next step: pair each video with a data point about lifecycle engagement results.
C2
The 2x2x2 attack messaging
It names a specific practice, declares it dead, and positions DO as the alternative. Expand this angle site-wide, and connect it to the data story: the reason 2x2x2 fails is that it ignores behavioral data and lifecycle timing.
C3
The "We make small teams feel huge" empathy line
This is the only line on the site that acknowledges the buyer's reality. People productization means putting a face behind the empathy. When Ted or Nate says this in a talk or a post, it carries more weight than anonymous site copy.

Bottom Line

The website has the raw materials of a high-converting site: named customer executives giving video testimonials, hard metrics across dozens of institutions, a differentiated attack angle (the 2x2x2 failure), a clear target buyer, and a leadership team (Ted, Nate, Adam) with deep domain credibility that the site currently hides.

What it lacks is a narrative built around two things: people and data. The site should position Ted, Nate, and Adam as visible guides with a point of view on data-driven lifecycle engagement. And it should tell the data story: DO's platform connects first-party behavioral data, automates targeting across the account holder lifecycle, and orchestrates engagement through digital banking and other channels. That story does not appear anywhere on the current site.

StoryBrand provides the framework to tell that story without a full redesign. The priority changes: rewrite the homepage hero around the data and lifecycle problem, build profile pages for Ted, Nate, and Adam, create a transitional CTA authored by a named leader (a gated report on data-driven engagement), fix the placeholder content, and add a three-step plan that names data, lifecycle, and digital banking explicitly.
