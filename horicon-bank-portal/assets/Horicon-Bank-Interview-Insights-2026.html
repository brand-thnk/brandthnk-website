What we heard across 12 calls

Primary Themes

01

Brand Awareness Gap in Growth Markets

The single most consistent finding: Horicon has a strong, trusted brand in legacy markets and is nearly invisible in Milwaukee and Fox Valley despite years of investment and, in Milwaukee's case, personal commitment from the CEO.

"People don't know Horicon Bank is here." Stephanie Albers, Erik Weis, Kerry Murphy

The tagline "The Natural Choice" is tied to the Horicon Marsh and resonates in legacy markets but carries no meaning in Milwaukee. "Together Doing Good" is gaining internal traction as a replacement — particularly in the southern region. Erik Weis uses it consistently in Milwaukee because "Natural Choice" draws blank stares.

Root cause note: The Milwaukee investment has a personal dimension that shapes the imbalance. Fred Jr. has lived in Milwaukee since 1973, commuted to Horicon since 1982, and his children work at and live near Milwaukee branches. The COVID-era branch opportunity was 1 mile from his house. This explains why legacy markets (Dodge County, Beaver Dam) — still the deposit base — feel the budget is skewed against them. This is a leadership conversation, not just a marketing one.
02

Persona Work Is Underway but Has Not Reached the People Who Need It

Personas exist, leadership has seen them, and the alignment around the framework is strong — described by multiple interviewees as unusual for this type of initiative. But they have not reached frontline staff. Branch teams still use demographic shorthand ("55 or better," "buying a house") because that's what they were trained on.

"Frontline and back office staff wouldn't recognize persona language." Cassie Voight

The current persona set is too large and too similar to drive action. The original Claritas-based personas (Wealthy Achievers, Flourishing Families, Upwardly Mobile) were designed for existing customer segmentation, not acquisition targeting. A commercial banking lens was missing entirely due to data source limitations. Strong recommendation across multiple voices: consolidate to 3–4 prioritized personas for both retail and commercial.

03

Deposit Growth Is the Dominant Strategic Priority — and It's Being Confused with Customer Age

Every stakeholder framed growth through deposits. But there is a specific internal misread happening: the directive to "grow deposits" is being interpreted by frontline staff as "we need younger customers." These are not the same thing.

"Staff interpreting 'need younger customers' — needs clarification. Should focus on share of wallet across all personas." Fred Schwertfeger Jr.

The most direct deposit gap: business clients who have loans with Horicon but no deposit accounts. Brenda Hetebrueg's southern region is already pursuing this — branch managers doing active business outreach is new behavior. The capture question is framed as: "What would it take to bring your full relationship to Horicon Bank?"

04

Technology Investment Without Execution — and There Are Real Numbers to Prove It

Total Expert has been in place 2–3 years. The good news from Danielle Schraufnagel: what's running is actually working. The problem is how little is running.

50–60%
Email open rates on active Total Expert journeys
56%
E-statement conversion rate (launched January 2026)
0%
Failure rate on Gliders (55+) journey — highest engagement segment

Despite these results, a 3-year wish list of journeys sits unbuilt. A youth checking e-statement journey is built and waiting for approval. Commercial onboarding doesn't exist yet. The blocker is not technical: it's the absence of a decision-maker with authority to move.

"Analysis paralysis in meetings prevents execution." Danielle Schraufnagel

Chrissy Oelke's framing of the broader pattern: the organization operates in "survival mode" rather than strategic mode. The persona data exists. The platform can execute persona-driven journeys without new investment — configuration work only.

05

Sales Culture in Transition — the Warmth Is an Asset, Not the Problem

The bank is culturally "order-takers." Known as the nicest people in town, not proactive solvers. Kerry Murphy put it directly: they help with what customers ask for, but don't anticipate future needs. The first-ever sales quotas were introduced last year.

"One teller generated 8 referrals in 1.5 weeks just by asking discovery questions." Brenda Hetebrueg

The southern region reached 25% of Q1 goal — framed by Brenda as a major improvement from where they were. The coaching need is not about making staff "salesy" — it's about giving them language to invite deeper relationships. The discovery question doing the most work: "What would it take to bring your full relationship to Horicon Bank?"

On the ground

Competitive Landscape by Region

The competitive challenge is not uniform. Each market requires a different response.

High Pressure

Fox Valley (Appleton / Oshkosh)

Credit unions dominate through rate competition and heavy radio advertising. Horicon cannot match rates. Brand awareness is minimal — "teeny tiny fish in a very big sea." Need: break through noise with consistency, not spend.

Building

Milwaukee / SE Wisconsin

3 years in market, 4–5 branches. People are still surprised Horicon has Milwaukee locations. No billboards. Digital ads not geo-tracked. Metrics are impressions, not conversions. Need: brand-building investment with measurable output.

Low Pressure

Legacy Markets (Dodge County / Ripon / Green Lake)

50% deposit share in Dodge County. Losses are almost entirely from customer relocation, not competition. Strong service reputation. The risk here is internal: legacy markets feel deprioritized relative to Milwaukee spend.

What's in the way

Issues to Address

Strategic

  • "Fear of missing out" prevents prioritization — spreading 5% effort across 20 initiatives vs. 50% on 2 (Traylor)
  • Milwaukee investment imbalance has a personal dimension — legacy markets are the deposit base but feel de-prioritized
  • No customer profitability measurement despite multiple attempts over multiple years (Traylor)
  • Deposit growth directive is being misread as "acquire younger customers" — needs explicit correction from leadership

Marketing & Operations

  • Budget is allocation-based, not strategic — ~$1.5M includes $500 branch parade donations; $300K went to credit card stock (ended up $150–180K)
  • No billboards in Milwaukee; digital ads not geo-tracked; metrics are impressions not conversions (Erik Weis)
  • Radio budget was cut and reallocated — credit unions now saturate Fox Valley airwaves
  • Operations excluded from Banking at Work design, creating manual processes that limit scale
  • Community donations skew to existing customers — "stingy" perception risk in new markets (Amy B.)

Leadership & Execution

  • No CMO or clear marketing decision-maker — Grace's departure left a gap that blocks execution
  • Total Expert renewed twice but underutilized; analysis paralysis in meetings is the specific blocker
  • Data insights from Chrissy's team not shared with frontline — branch staff can't act on intelligence they can't see
  • Org operates in "survival mode" vs. strategic mode (Chrissy Oelke)
  • External consultants repeatedly chosen over internal expertise, creating buyer's remorse pattern (Traylor)

Who they're serving

Persona Insights

Business Owner

Primary commercial target. Loans often exist; deposits don't. Finds Horicon attractive once aware. Conversion rate is strong once prospects engage — the barrier is reach and ask, not fit.

Opportunity: Full-relationship capture; Banking at Work; Treasury management fee waivers as entry point

Established Steward / Senior (55–74)

Strongest existing segment. Zero churn on Total Expert journey. Trust-oriented, prefers in-person. Responds to estate planning, events, and community presence. Milwaukee has minimal senior program competition — an open lane.

Opportunity: Senior expansion in Milwaukee; Gliders event outreach to prospects (not just existing customers); funeral trust and assisted living partnerships

Growing Family / First-Time Homebuyer

Horicon has mortgage strength that isn't translating to brand awareness. "Best bank for mortgages" but not top-of-mind for home buyers. Proactive outreach at the mortgage moment is the window.

Opportunity: Mortgage marketing; Banking at Work employer partnerships; school-based financial literacy

First-Gen / Youth (Under 25)

Real competitors are Chime, Cash App, and GreenLight — not other banks. Financial literacy gap starts in high school. "My Money" checking gives Horicon an entry point if it can reach students before habits form. Cassie's own 16-year-old (who works at the bank) has zero financial knowledge.

Opportunity: "My Money" checking; high school visits and partnerships; digital-first engagement; financial literacy curriculum

Contrarian View — Robert Traylor (EVP / CPA)

The argument: Focus on Boomers + Gen Z. Skip Millennials — they want digital speed and product breadth Horicon cannot match. Gen Z, counterintuitively, is showing preference for their parents' and grandparents' banking approaches — more traditional, more trust-based. This is the "bookend strategy."

Why it's worth taking seriously: Traylor is the most analytically rigorous voice in the engagement. His Gen Z observation is consistent with emerging behavioral data. The underlying principle — concentrate resources where you can actually win — is sound regardless of whether the specific segmentation holds.

What it needs: Validation against Horicon's own customer acquisition data before acting on it. If Millennial acquisition cost is materially higher than other segments, the argument strengthens considerably.
