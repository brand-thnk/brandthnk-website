01

Write campaign copy that lands on the first try

Layer 8 / Messaging Receptivity
The Problem

Most fintech marketing teams write copy based on internal assumptions about what their buyers care about. They iterate through rounds of A/B tests, slowly converging on language that works, burning budget and time on every cycle. The feedback loop is slow and expensive.

How Litmus Fixes It

Layer 8 of every Litmus persona documents three things: language that resonates, language that repels, and objection patterns. These aren't guesses. They're patterns observed across 50+ workshops with the exact buyers you're trying to reach. You start with copy that's tested against real behavioral data, not invented in a brainstorm.

Language that resonates

  • "We work with 40 community banks your size across the Southeast."
  • "This integrates natively with Fiserv Premier. No middleware required."
  • "You will not need to hire additional staff to manage this."

Language that repels

  • "Disrupt your legacy infrastructure."
  • "AI-powered" without a provable use case.
  • "Move fast and break things."
  • "Our Series C funding..."
How to Apply It
1

Identify your target persona. Match the prospect's title, institution type, and asset size to a Litmus persona.

2

Pull Layer 8. Read the resonance and repel language along with the objection patterns for that persona.

3

Draft with the grain. Write copy using the phrases and framing that align with how this buyer processes trust signals.

4

Audit against repel language. Check every line against the repel list. Remove anything that triggers skepticism before it ships.

02

Allocate channel spend by persona behavior

Channel Behavioral Modes
The Problem

Marketing teams distribute budget across channels based on industry benchmarks or past performance. But the same buyer processes the same message completely differently depending on where they see it. A LinkedIn ad and a website landing page aren't just different formats. They activate different psychological modes.

The Four Channel Modes

LinkedIn: Peer-Visibility

Buyers share what makes them look informed to peers. Content that doesn't serve this function gets scrolled past. Your ad needs to feel like something they'd want to be seen engaging with.

Website: Due Diligence

Every claim on your site gets matched against what the buyer already knows about their own institution. Vague promises fail instantly. Specificity around core platform, bank size, and region is the only credibility signal.

Thought Leadership: Learning

Buyers are open to ideas but hostile to disguised sales. The test: can they use what you taught them tomorrow, even if they never buy from you? If yes, you earn trust. If not, you lose it.

Ads: Interruption

Two seconds. Highest skepticism of any channel. Generic value propositions die here. The only thing that earns a click is painful specificity: their core platform, their asset range, their exact problem.

How to Apply It

For each target persona, check which channel mode aligns with where they are in the buying journey. A CEO in early exploration needs thought leadership (learning mode). The same CEO evaluating you needs your website (due diligence mode). Match the channel to the mode, not to the funnel stage.

03

Arm sales with real buyer context before every call

Layers 5-7 / Psychographics, Pain Points, Buying Context
The Problem

Most discovery calls are generic. The rep asks the same qualification questions regardless of who they're talking to, and the buyer, who has sat through dozens of these calls, checks out. The information gap between what the rep knows and what the buyer expects them to know is where deals stall.

What Layers 5-7 Give You

Layer 5: Psychographics

  • Risk tolerance and decision style
  • Trust signals (peer references vs. data vs. demos)
  • Information diet: where they learn, who they listen to
  • What drives change (competitive fear vs. board pressure vs. opportunity)

Layer 6: Pain Points

  • Top operational challenges ranked by urgency
  • Platform frustrations they live with daily
  • Competitive anxieties keeping them up at night
  • Board-level friction points they can't ignore

Example: Before calling a community bank CEO on Fiserv Premier, your rep already knows: the contract renews in 8 months, the board chair forwarded a competitor's app ad asking "why don't we have this?", and the CEO won't move without calling three peer CEOs first. That's a conversation between people who already speak the same language.

How to Apply It
1

Match the persona pre-call. Use title, institution type, and asset size to find the right Litmus profile.

2

Build the one-pager. Pull Layer 5 psychographics, Layer 6 pain points, and Layer 7 buying context into a pre-call brief.

3

Lead with what you know. Open with a pain point, not a pitch. "We've seen banks your size on Premier struggling with [specific challenge]. Is that landing for you?"

04

Build content for real people, not abstract personas

Layers 1-3 / Identity, Institution Profile, Role Authority
The Problem

Content teams write for "decision-makers in financial services" — an audience so broad it connects with no one. The resulting thought leadership is generic enough to apply to any institution of any size in any market, which means it's specific enough for none of them.

What Layers 1-3 Unlock

These layers give you the biographical and institutional specificity to write for a real person. You know their career arc (Layer 1), their institution's size, charter type, and customer mix (Layer 2), and their actual authority: budget thresholds, board relationships, who reports to whom (Layer 3).

Generic content

"Digital transformation is a priority for financial institutions looking to stay competitive in today's market."

Litmus-informed content

"If you're a community bank CEO on Fiserv Premier with a contract renewal in the next 12 months, your board is probably already asking why your mobile app looks like it was built in 2018."

Content Types This Powers
Blog posts and articles

Written for a specific persona's career stage and institutional reality, not an abstract role.

Case studies

Framed around the exact pain points and buying context of the persona you're targeting, not a generic win story.

Webinar topics

Chosen based on what each persona type is actually trying to learn, per their information diet in Layer 5.

Email sequences

Written to reference specific technology stacks, regulatory pressures, and competitive dynamics, by segment.

05

Prioritize ABM accounts by timing and fit

Layers 4 & 7 / Tech Landscape, Buying Context
The Problem

ABM programs target accounts by firmographic fit: asset size, geography, institution type. But firmographics don't tell you whether an account is ready to buy. A $5B bank that just signed a 7-year core contract is a bad target regardless of how well it fits your ICP on paper.

The Timing Signals in Litmus

Layer 4: Tech Landscape

  • Core platform and contract status
  • Digital banking vendor and satisfaction level
  • Cloud posture and API readiness
  • Known integration pain points

Layer 7: Buying Context

  • Active trigger events: renewal, board pressure, competitive threat
  • Budget cycle and annual tech spend range
  • Buying committee composition
  • Dealbreaker criteria: integration, references, compliance
How to Apply It
1

Score for timing. Overlay Litmus trigger events onto your target list. Accounts with converging triggers (contract renewal plus board pressure plus competitive threat) go to the top.

2

Score for fit. Match Layer 4 tech landscape to your integration strengths. If your product requires API readiness and the account has none, deprioritize.

3

Check for dealbreakers. Layer 7 documents what kills deals for each persona type. If you can't meet a dealbreaker criterion, don't spend the outreach.

4

Sequence by buying cycle. Use budget cycle data to time your outreach to the quarter when tech evaluations actually happen, not when your pipeline needs filling.

The result: Instead of 200 accounts getting the same sequence, you have 40 accounts getting precisely timed, precisely messaged outreach based on where they actually are in their buying cycle, and 160 accounts parked until their timing improves.
