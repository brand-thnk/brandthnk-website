Three Types of Acquisitions: Why We Chose the Operator's Path

When looking at the landscape of companies to acquire, there are three strategic approaches. Here's why we landed where we did:

1. House of Brands Strategy

When: Strong brand recognition deserves its own room

Example: If we acquired a company with established industry presence (shows up in major analyst reports, industry events, or well-known thought leadership), we'd create a house of brands. Like adding a fully decorated room to HC3—it slots right in with its own identity.

Why we didn't: These acquisitions are extremely pricey and few companies have that level of brand equity. Plus, most available targets don't meet this threshold.

2. Platform Strategy

When: End-to-end technology you couldn't build in reasonable time

Example: A complete core banking solution or comprehensive compliance suite that becomes "HC3 Platform"—customers buy the platform, then add modules like statements, payments, risk management.

Why we didn't: Requires decoupling all solutions so they can be purchased as modules. Significant engineering work. More targets available, but still complex integration.

3. Micro Solutions / Sticky Note Problems

When: Highly specialized companies that fill operational gaps

Example: Companies like Auto ABS—focused on one specific high-stakes operational challenge that FIs struggle with internally. Often surprisingly profitable with solid revenue despite narrow focus.

Why we chose this: More acquisitions available, reasonable pricing, fits our operator's mindset. These usually build toward platform strategy over time—you buy the wedge, then expand the offering.

The Strategic Path Forward

We're not locked into one approach forever. The typical progression is: Micro solutions → Platform strategy → House of brands. Each acquisition builds toward the next level as you gain cash, capabilities, and market position.
